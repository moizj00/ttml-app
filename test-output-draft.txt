## II. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### A. Material Breach and Abandonment

Your conduct constitutes a **material breach** of the Contract under California law. The California Supreme Court has held that a material breach is one that "goes to the essence of the contract" and defeats the purpose of the agreement, thereby excusing further performance by the non-breaching party and entitling her to seek damages. *Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman*, 51 Cal.4th 811, 821 (2011).

Here, the essence of the Contract was the complete renovation of Ms. Mitchell's kitchen by July 15, 2025, in exchange for $85,000. Your abandonment of the project at only 30% completion—followed by complete non-responsiveness for over eight months—constitutes a total failure of performance that goes to the very heart of the contractual relationship. Under *Oasis West Realty*, this material breach entitles Ms. Mitchell to terminate the Contract, recover damages, and pursue all available remedies.

Your unilateral cessation of work and refusal to communicate further evidences a clear **repudiation** of your contractual obligations, triggering Ms. Mitchell's right to treat the Contract as terminated and seek full compensatory relief.

### B. Damages Recoverable Under California Law

California Civil Code Section 3300 provides that a party injured by breach of contract is entitled to recover "the amount which will compensate the party aggrieved for all the detriment proximately caused thereby, or which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result therefrom."

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to the following categories of damages:

**1. Refund of Unearned Deposit**

Of the $42,500 deposit paid, Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has earned compensation only for the approximately 30% of work actually performed. Applying a proportional calculation:

- Value of work completed (30% of $85,000): $25,500
- Unearned portion of deposit: **$42,500 − $25,500 = $17,000**

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to immediate refund of at least **$17,000** representing the unearned deposit. However, given the defective and incomplete nature of the work performed, Ms. Mitchell reserves the right to contest the valuation of work completed and seek additional recovery.

**2. Cost to Complete**

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to recover the cost of hiring a replacement contractor to complete the remaining 70% of the contracted work, less the unpaid balance of the original contract price. Civil Code Section 3300; *see also* Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 347. Ms. Mitchell is currently obtaining bids from licensed contractors and will supplement this demand with specific figures upon receipt of those bids.

**3. Consequential Damages**

The $3,200 Ms. Mitchell has expended on temporary kitchen arrangements constitutes foreseeable consequential damages recoverable under Civil Code Section 3300. It is entirely foreseeable that abandonment of a kitchen renovation mid-project would render the kitchen unusable and necessitate alternative arrangements. *See* Civil Code § 3300 (damages "which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result" from breach).

**4. Prejudgment Interest**

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to prejudgment interest at the rate of **ten percent (10%) per annum** on all damages from the date of breach (May 20, 2025) through the date of judgment. Cal. Civ. Code § 3289(b).

**5. Attorney's Fees**

To the extent the Contract contains an attorney's fees provision, Ms. Mitchell will seek recovery of all reasonable attorney's fees incurred in enforcing her rights. Cal. Civ. Code § 1717.

### C. Alternative Remedy: Rescission and Restitution

In the alternative, Ms. Mitchell may elect to rescind the Contract pursuant to California Civil Code Section 1689(b)(4), which permits rescission where a party's performance is "prevented or rendered substantially impracticable" by the other party's breach. Upon rescission, Ms. Mitchell would be entitled to full restitution of the $42,500 deposit, subject to offset for any value conferred.

### D. Statute of Limitations

This claim is timely. The applicable statute of limitations for breach of a written contract is **four years** under California Code of Civil Procedure Section 337(a). The breach occurred on May 20, 2025, when Pacific Coast Contractors LLC abandoned the project. Fewer than twelve months have elapsed, and Ms. Mitchell's claims remain fully actionable.

## III. RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED DEFENSES

We anticipate that Pacific Coast Contractors LLC may attempt to assert the following defenses, each of which is without merit:

**1. "Partial performance justifies retention of the deposit."**

While a contractor may be entitled to quantum meruit recovery for work actually performed, this does not excuse the material breach or justify retention of funds in excess of the value provided. Ms. Mitchell has already accounted for the estimated value of work completed ($25,500) in calculating the unearned deposit. Any claim to additional compensation must be supported by documentation of actual labor and materials costs, which Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has failed to provide despite ample opportunity.

**2. "The homeowner failed to mitigate damages."**

Ms. Mitchell has acted reasonably and in good faith throughout this dispute. She made repeated efforts to contact Pacific Coast Contractors LLC to resolve the matter before seeking alternative contractors. California law requires only *reasonable* mitigation efforts, not immediate replacement of a breaching contractor while the homeowner is still attempting to salvage the contractual relationship. Ms. Mitchell's conduct has been exemplary.

**3. "Force majeure or owner-caused delays excused performance."**

The facts demonstrate unilateral abandonment by Pacific Coast Contractors LLC, not delay caused by external circumstances or the homeowner. No force majeure event has been identified, and Ms. Mitchell did not interfere with or delay the work in any manner. This defense is wholly inapplicable.

**4. "The mediation clause bars litigation."**

While the Contract contains a mediation provision, Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has made mediation impossible through its complete refusal to respond to any communications. A party cannot invoke a dispute resolution clause as a shield after rendering that process futile through its own non-responsiveness. Ms. Mitchell has satisfied any obligation to attempt pre-litigation resolution; Pacific Coast Contractors LLC's silence constitutes waiver of the mediation requirement.

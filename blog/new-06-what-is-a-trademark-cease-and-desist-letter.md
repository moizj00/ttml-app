# What Is a Trademark Cease and Desist Letter and When Does It Work?

If someone is using your brand name, logo, or product design without authorization, a trademark cease and desist letter is often the fastest and most cost-effective tool to make it stop.

Here's exactly what that letter is, what it contains, and when it tends to be effective.

## What Is a Trademark?

A trademark is any word, name, symbol, slogan, or combination of those that identifies the source of goods or services and distinguishes them from others. In the US, trademark rights arise from actual use in commerce — but federal registration with the USPTO (US Patent and Trademark Office) gives you considerably stronger enforcement tools, including the right to sue in federal court and the ability to use the ® symbol.

California also has a state trademark registration process through the Secretary of State, which provides limited protection for commerce conducted solely within California.

## What Does a Trademark Cease and Desist Letter Contain?

A well-drafted trademark C&D letter typically includes:

**Your rights:** Registration number(s) or a description of your common-law rights, plus how long and in what channels you've been using the mark.

**Description of the infringement:** Specific details — the listing URL, product photos, or business name — showing how the other party is using your mark.

**Legal basis:** A reference to federal trademark law (15 U.S.C. § 1114 for registered marks, § 1125(a) for unregistered marks and false designation of origin) and, where applicable, California's Unfair Competition Law (Business & Professions Code §17200).

**Demands:** Typically: stop using the mark, pull listings, destroy infringing inventory, and provide written confirmation of compliance. Sometimes also: transfer a domain name, pay damages, or account for profits.

**Deadline:** Usually 10–30 days for a written response.

**Reservation of rights:** A statement that you reserve all legal remedies if they don't comply.

## When Trademark Cease and Desist Letters Work Best

These letters are most effective in certain situations:

**When the infringer is a real business.** A company with employees, a physical address, and revenue has something to lose. Legal exposure from trademark litigation is a serious deterrent.

**When the infringement is clear.** If you have a registered mark and someone is using it on nearly identical goods in the same category, there's little room for the infringer to argue innocence.

**When the infringer is smaller than you.** Larger companies may have legal teams prepared to push back. Smaller operators — individual sellers, new brands — are much more likely to comply when they receive an attorney letter.

**When it's sent by an attorney.** Letters on firm letterhead, signed by a licensed attorney, signal that you have counsel and are prepared to follow through. Template letters you write yourself rarely carry the same weight.

## When a C&D May Not Be Enough

Cease and desist letters aren't a universal solution:

- International sellers operating anonymously may ignore a letter with limited consequences.
- A large, well-funded infringer may treat it as the opening move in litigation and respond with a counter-letter arguing non-infringement.
- Sellers who believe their use is a fair use or parody may push back on principled grounds.

In those cases, escalating to a trademark infringement lawsuit or combining the letter with a platform takedown report may be necessary. For a breakdown of when to escalate, see our [demand letter vs. lawsuit comparison](/blog/new-03-demand-letter-vs-lawsuit).

## Why California Context Matters

For California-based businesses, an attorney's letter can leverage both federal trademark law and California's Unfair Competition Law (Business & Professions Code §17200), which prohibits unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts and practices. Citing both bodies of law can broaden your legal leverage against an infringer.

California courts also allow for disgorgement of profits in unfair competition cases, which can be meaningful if the infringer has made substantial sales using your mark.

## When a Trademark Letter Is the Right First Move

For most California businesses facing brand copying or trademark infringement, a cease and desist letter from a licensed attorney is the right first step — it's faster, cheaper, and less disruptive than litigation, and it often works.

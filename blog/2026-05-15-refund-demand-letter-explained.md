# What Is a Refund Demand Letter and When Should You Send One?

A refund demand letter is a formal written request telling a business to return your money by a specific deadline — or face legal consequences. It's not a lawsuit. It's not a threat. It's the recognized legal step that comes before either of those things, and in most consumer disputes, it's the step that actually resolves the problem.

## How a Refund Demand Letter Works

The letter serves three purposes at once. It documents your claim in writing, which matters if the dispute ever reaches a courtroom. It demonstrates that you attempted to resolve the issue before filing suit — something California small claims judges look for. And it forces the business to make a calculation: is it cheaper to refund you or to fight?

For most small-to-mid-range consumer disputes — defective products, services never rendered, deposits not returned — the answer is almost always that the refund is cheaper. That's why demand letters have such a high resolution rate. The business isn't responding to your frustration. It's responding to documented legal exposure.
## What Goes Into a Refund Demand Letter

A strong refund demand letter has a specific structure. It opens by identifying you and the transaction — what you bought, when, how much you paid, and from whom. It then describes the problem clearly and factually: the product arrived damaged, the service was never performed, the warranty repair was refused without grounds.

Next comes the demand itself. State the exact dollar amount you want returned and give a reasonable deadline — 15 to 30 days is standard in California. Anything shorter can look unreasonable to a judge; anything longer gives the business room to stall.

The letter should reference the legal basis for your claim. If the business misrepresented the product, cite the California Consumer Legal Remedies Act (Civil Code Section 1750 et seq.). If the store had no posted return policy, cite Civil Code Section 1723. If a warranty was breached, cite the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. You don't need to write a legal brief — one or two sentences identifying the statute is enough to signal that you know the law.

Finally, state what happens if the business doesn't comply. The standard language is something like: "If I do not receive the requested refund by [date], I intend to pursue all available legal remedies, including filing in California Small Claims Court." Direct, factual, no bluffing.

## When You Should Send One

Send a refund demand letter when you've already tried the normal channels and been refused. If you called customer service three times and got nowhere, if the store manager said no, if your emails went unanswered — that's when the demand letter becomes the right tool.

Common scenarios where a refund demand letter is appropriate include a business that charged you for services it didn't deliver, a retailer that sold you a defective product and refused to honor the return policy, a gym or subscription service that keeps charging after you canceled, a contractor who took a deposit and never started work, and an online seller who shipped something materially different from what was advertised.

In each case, the informal resolution path has failed, and the formal one hasn't started yet. The demand letter is the bridge.
## When You Probably Don't Need One

If the business has a clear return policy and you're within the window, start there. If your credit card company can handle the chargeback (typically within 60 days of the charge under the Fair Credit Billing Act), that may be faster. A demand letter is most valuable when the simple options have been exhausted or when the amount is significant enough that you want a documented legal record.

## Why It Works Better Than You'd Expect

People often assume that a letter won't change anything — that if calling didn't work, writing won't either. But a demand letter operates on a different level. Phone calls are informal and deniable. A demand letter is a legal document. It tells the business that the next step is a courthouse, and it gives them a specific, time-limited off-ramp.

In California, small claims court filing fees range from $30 to $75 depending on the amount. The business knows that if you've gone to the trouble of writing a formal demand letter with statutory citations, you're likely to follow through. Most would rather issue the refund than spend a half-day in court defending a losing position.

# Someone Is Selling Knockoffs of My Product on Amazon — What Can I Do in California?

You built your product from scratch — and now a stranger is undercutting you on Amazon with a cheap copy. It's infuriating, and it's costing you sales and customer trust.

The short answer: you have real options. A combination of platform reports, formal legal letters, and copyright or trademark tools can put significant pressure on an infringing seller. Here's what to know.

## Step One: Document Everything Before You Act

Before taking any action, capture screenshots of the infringing listing — the product photos, title, description, price, seller name, and ASIN. Note how the knockoff resembles your product, and save any customer reviews mentioning they received a fake or inferior item.

This documentation isn't just helpful — it's essential if you escalate. Courts and platforms both want specifics, not general complaints.

## Understand What Legal Rights You Have

Your enforcement options depend on what protections you've established:

**Trademark:** If you own a registered trademark on your brand name, logo, or product design, you have strong legal standing. Using your mark without authorization on competing goods is likely trademark infringement under federal law.

**Copyright:** Original creative elements — packaging artwork, product photographs, written copy — receive copyright protection automatically upon creation in the US. Copying those elements can constitute infringement even without registration, though registration strengthens your enforcement position considerably.

**Patent:** If your product has a utility or design patent, an unauthorized copycat may be infringing it.

**No registration yet?** California's Unfair Competition Law (Business & Professions Code §17200) and federal common-law trademark rights can still provide a basis for action if you've been using your mark in commerce. But registered rights are significantly easier to enforce quickly.

## Option 1: Amazon Brand Registry and Platform Infringement Reports

Amazon Brand Registry is available to sellers with a registered trademark. It gives you access to proactive brand protection tools and a more streamlined infringement reporting system. Amazon can remove listings, issue warnings, and in some cases suspend repeat offenders.

Even without Brand Registry enrollment, Amazon's standard infringement reporting tool lets you flag violations. Include your trademark or copyright registration numbers where available. Platform reports can result in removals within days, though outcomes vary.

## Option 2: Send a Cease and Desist Letter

A formal attorney-reviewed cease and desist letter puts the infringing seller on direct legal notice. It identifies your rights, describes the specific infringement, demands they stop selling the knockoffs, and gives a deadline to respond.

Why it matters beyond Amazon's report form:

- It creates a paper trail that can be used in litigation.
- Many sellers comply when they receive a letter from an attorney — especially small operations that want to avoid legal exposure.
- It addresses the infringer directly, not just one platform, which matters if they're active on multiple sites.
- For California-based infringers, a letter from a California attorney can invoke both federal trademark law and state unfair competition claims.

For a broader overview of how these letters work and when they're effective, see our [guide to cease and desist letters](/blog/new-02-cease-and-desist-letters-explained).

## Option 3: DMCA Takedown (for Copyright Claims)

If your original product photos, packaging artwork, or written descriptions are being copied, you can file a DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act) takedown notice directly with Amazon. This is a formal legal mechanism under federal law. Amazon must act on a valid notice promptly. Note that submitting a false or inaccurate DMCA notice creates legal liability for the sender — make sure your claim is accurate before filing.

## Option 4: Litigation (Last Resort)

Filing a lawsuit is expensive and slow, and rarely cost-effective for low-dollar disputes. For significant revenue loss or serious brand damage, federal trademark or copyright infringement litigation can result in injunctions, actual damages, and in some cases statutory damages and attorney's fees. Most attorneys recommend exhausting platform tools and demand letters before considering this path.

## When a Letter Is the Right First Tool

If you have trademark or copyright protections in place and want to stop a knockoff seller without committing to expensive litigation, an attorney-reviewed cease and desist letter is usually the fastest and most affordable first step.

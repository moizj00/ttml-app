# Attorney Letter vs. DMCA Takedown: Which Gets Counterfeit Listings Removed Faster?

Your product is being copied and sold online without your permission. You know you have enforcement options — but should you file a DMCA takedown notice, send a cease and desist letter, or both?

This is one of the most practical questions California ecommerce sellers face. Here's a side-by-side breakdown so you can choose the right tool — or the right combination.

## What Each Tool Actually Is

**DMCA Takedown Notice**

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (17 U.S.C. § 512) is a federal law that gives copyright owners a formal mechanism to request that online platforms remove infringing content. You submit a sworn notice to the platform — Amazon, Etsy, YouTube, Instagram, or any other that participates — identifying the infringing material, your copyright claim, and a good-faith declaration that the use is unauthorized.

A platform that receives a valid, complete DMCA notice must act on it promptly ("expeditiously" under the statute) to maintain its safe harbor from liability. The alleged infringer can file a counter-notice, after which the platform may restore the content within 10–14 business days unless you file a lawsuit.

Important: submitting a materially false DMCA notice creates legal liability for the sender under 17 U.S.C. § 512(f). Make sure your copyright claim is accurate before filing.

**Cease and Desist Letter**

An attorney-drafted cease and desist letter goes directly to the infringer — not the platform. It puts them on formal legal notice, identifies your rights, describes the specific infringement, and demands they stop. It doesn't trigger any automatic platform removal, but it creates documented legal exposure for the infringer and often prompts voluntary compliance across all channels simultaneously.

For a full overview of how cease and desist letters work, see our [guide to cease and desist letters](/blog/new-02-cease-and-desist-letters-explained).

## When to Use Each Tool

**Reach for a DMCA Takedown when:**
- The infringement is clearly copyright-based: someone copied your product photos, original written descriptions, or unique artwork
- You want fast, platform-enforced removal with a formal legal mechanism
- The infringer's identity is unknown or hard to contact directly
- You need the listing down quickly and can submit a compliant notice

**Reach for a Cease and Desist Letter when:**
- Your claim is trademark-based, not (or not only) copyright-based
- The infringer is active across multiple platforms and you want one action to address all of them
- You want to build a documented legal record for potential litigation
- You want to signal serious legal intent directly to the infringer
- The infringer is a business with identifiable contact information

**Use Both when:**
Many experienced ecommerce sellers take a two-track approach: send the cease and desist letter to the infringer while simultaneously filing DMCA notices or trademark infringement reports with the relevant platforms. This maximizes pressure and covers both the platform removal and the direct-to-infringer legal notice.

## The Speed Question, Answered Honestly

DMCA takedowns can produce listing removals within 24–72 hours on responsive platforms like Amazon — *if* your notice is complete and valid. However, if the infringer files a counter-notice, their listing may be restored within 10–14 business days unless you follow through with a federal lawsuit.

Cease and desist letters typically draw a response within 7–30 days. Some infringers comply immediately; others want to negotiate; some ignore the letter. When ignored, the paper trail the letter creates becomes valuable evidence.

So: **DMCA is often faster for getting a specific listing down on one platform.** A cease and desist letter is often more durable — it addresses the infringer's behavior across all channels and creates legal exposure that a platform report alone does not.

## What California Sellers Should Know

California's Unfair Competition Law (Business & Professions Code §17200) gives California-based businesses additional tools beyond federal copyright and trademark protections. An attorney who practices in California can draft a cease and desist that invokes both federal and state claims, which may increase leverage over an infringer — particularly one also based in California.

For trademark claims specifically, California courts can award disgorgement of profits earned through unfair competition, which is a meaningful remedy if the infringer has made substantial sales using your brand.

## The Practical Bottom Line

For most ecommerce sellers dealing with counterfeit goods or copied listings, the most effective approach is:

1. File a trademark infringement report or DMCA notice with the platform for fast listing removal.
2. At the same time, send an attorney-reviewed cease and desist letter directly to the infringer.
3. If neither produces results, consult an attorney about whether formal litigation makes financial sense.

# Cease and Desist Letters for Ecommerce Sellers Facing Counterfeit Goods

You built a product people love. Now a copycat is selling a knockoff version on Amazon, Etsy, or their own Shopify store — and your brand reputation is taking the hit.

A cease and desist letter is one of the primary tools ecommerce sellers use to address this. Here's how it works in practice, and what to expect when you send one.

## Why Counterfeiting Hurts More Than Just Sales

Lost revenue is the obvious damage, but copycat products create problems that go deeper:

- **Negative reviews on your listings.** When a customer buys a counterfeit from another seller and receives an inferior product, they sometimes leave that review on the original brand's page, not the knockoff seller's. Your star rating suffers for someone else's bad product.
- **Customer trust erosion.** Buyers who receive fakes and don't realize it yet may lose confidence in your brand. Those who do realize it feel deceived and rarely return.
- **Safety exposure.** If a counterfeit version of your product causes injury — especially in categories like electronics, supplements, or children's goods — the legal implications can extend to the original manufacturer depending on how the listing is structured.

## What a Cease and Desist Letter Does for Ecommerce Sellers

For online sellers, a C&D letter serves several specific functions:

**Creates formal legal notice.** Once you've put someone on written notice of their infringement, they can no longer claim ignorance. That matters if the dispute escalates.

**Prompts listing removal.** Many counterfeit sellers know they're operating in legally gray territory. A letter from a California attorney that cites specific trademark or copyright registrations often results in listings being pulled promptly — especially from smaller operators who don't want legal exposure.

**Opens a path to resolution.** Some infringers will reach out after receiving a letter to negotiate. This can result in payment, a brand change, or simply a commitment to stop selling the product.

**Strengthens platform complaints.** A formal cease and desist supports your Amazon infringement report or DMCA notice. It documents that you've taken independent legal steps, which can matter if the platform complaint is disputed.

## Common Ecommerce Scenarios That Call for a C&D Letter

**Counterfeit branded products:** Someone is using your registered trademark on products they manufacture or resell without authorization.

**Design copying on print-on-demand platforms:** A competitor is lifting your original artwork and selling it on T-shirts, mugs, or phone cases through Redbubble, Printify, or similar services.

**Brand name confusion:** A new seller launched under a name confusingly similar to yours in the same product category.

**Listing hijacking:** Another seller has attached their offer to your ASIN and is selling counterfeit goods to your own customers.

**Product photography theft:** Your professional product photos are being used in competitor listings without permission — this is a copyright issue, not just a business dispute.

## What an Effective Letter Should Include

For ecommerce infringement, the letter should:

- Identify your specific trademark or copyright registration(s), or describe your common-law rights
- Point to the infringing listings by ASIN, URL, or product identifier
- Explain the legal rights being violated under federal trademark or copyright law, plus California Business & Professions Code §17200 where applicable
- Demand removal of listings, destruction or return of infringing inventory, and cessation of sales
- Set a specific response deadline (typically 10–21 days)
- Reserve your right to seek damages, injunctive relief, or other remedies if they don't comply

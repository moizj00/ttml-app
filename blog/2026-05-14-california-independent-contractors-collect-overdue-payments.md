# How California Independent Contractors Can Collect Overdue Payments Without Going to Court

You finished the project. The client signed off. The invoice went out. And then — nothing. If you're a California independent contractor chasing an unpaid bill, the good news is you have more leverage than most people realize, and you don't need to sue anyone to use it.

**Short answer:** Most overdue payment disputes resolve with a well-structured escalation: document the debt, send a formal demand letter, and give the client a real deadline. In California, the combination of strong contract law and an attorney-signed demand resolves the majority of cases before anyone files anything in court.

## Step 1: Get Your Documentation in Order

Before you do anything else, gather:

- Your signed contract or written agreement (or email chain confirming scope and rate)
- The invoice, including the due date and amount
- Any evidence of delivery — submitted files, emails confirming completion, client acknowledgment showing they accepted the work
- A record of any previous payment reminders you've sent

This documentation is the backbone of your claim. If you end up sending a demand letter or filing in small claims, every item above becomes evidence. For a full breakdown of what a demand letter should contain, see our guide on [what is a demand letter](/blog/new-01-what-is-a-demand-letter).

## Step 2: Make One Clear, Documented Informal Request

Before escalating to a formal demand, send one final email that makes the situation explicit: "Invoice #123 for $3,500 was due April 15. As of today it remains unpaid. Please confirm when you will submit payment." Keep it professional, not emotional. Copy yourself on all communications.

If the client responds and gives a clear timeline, document it. If they don't respond or keep stalling, you've established a pattern of avoidance — which matters if this goes further.

## Step 3: Send an Attorney-Drafted Demand Letter

This is the step most independent contractors skip too quickly. They send a few emails, get frustrated, and either give up or jump straight to filing in small claims court. But a formal demand letter — drafted and signed by an attorney — is often all it takes.

Here's why it works: it changes how the client perceives the cost of not paying. A personal email is easy to ignore. A letter on law firm letterhead with a 10-day payment deadline and a statement that civil litigation will follow is much harder to dismiss.

Under California law, independent contractors have the right to enforce written contracts and implied contracts for completed services. If you performed the work and the client accepted it, the debt exists regardless of whether the client now disputes the invoice. The demand letter puts them on notice that you understand this and are prepared to act.

## Step 4: Know Your California-Specific Options

California gives independent contractors several escalation paths if the demand letter doesn't produce results:

**Small claims court:** For amounts up to $12,500 (for individuals), this is the fastest formal option. Filing fees are $30–$100 depending on the amount. You don't need an attorney to appear. Hearings typically happen within 20–70 days of filing.

**Limited civil court:** For amounts between $12,500 and $35,000, this is the next tier. A bit more formal, but still manageable for straightforward payment disputes.

**Collections:** Turning the debt over to a collections agency is another option, though you'll typically give up 25–40% of what's recovered. Worth considering for smaller amounts where court time isn't worth it.

**Mechanics' lien (for construction contractors):** If you worked on real property, you may be able to file a mechanic's lien, which clouds the property title until you're paid. This is a powerful tool specific to contractors who improved real property in California.

## Step 5: Don't Wait Too Long

California's statute of limitations for written contract claims is four years (CCP § 337). But practical collection gets harder the longer you wait. A client who's 45 days late is much easier to collect from than one who's 18 months late. Send the demand letter while the project is still fresh and your documentation is complete.

When a letter is the right tool: once informal reminders have failed, a formal attorney demand letter is the fastest and most cost-effective way to collect before going to court.

# What Is a Consumer Demand Letter and When Should You Send One?

A consumer demand letter is a formal written notice sent to a business that owes you money or has failed to deliver a product or service. It states what you're owed, why you're owed it, and what legal action you'll pursue if the matter isn't resolved by a specific deadline. Think of it as the last serious step before a courtroom.

**The short answer:** A consumer demand letter is a legally significant document — not just a strongly worded complaint. In California, sending one before filing a lawsuit is often required under the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (Civil Code Section 1782), and it's almost always strategically smart even when it isn't legally required.

## How Is a Consumer Demand Letter Different from a Complaint Email?

The difference is legal weight. A complaint email tells a business you're unhappy. A demand letter tells a business you've identified a specific legal obligation they've failed to meet, you've calculated the amount owed, and you're prepared to enforce your rights in court if the deadline passes.

When that letter comes from a licensed attorney, the signal is even stronger. It tells the business that you've already consulted legal counsel — which means the next piece of mail they receive could be a court filing.

Businesses process demand letters differently than customer complaints. A complaint goes to customer service. A demand letter goes to the legal department or the owner. That routing alone often produces results that months of emails and phone calls couldn't.

## When Should You Send a Consumer Demand Letter?

A demand letter makes sense when three conditions are met: you have a clear legal basis for your claim, you've already tried to resolve the issue informally, and the amount at stake justifies the effort.

Common situations where consumers in California send demand letters include a business refusing to refund money for a service never performed, a company delivering a defective product and refusing a return, a contractor abandoning a home improvement project mid-work, a gym or membership service continuing to charge after cancellation, and a landlord wrongfully withholding a security deposit.

If you've sent at least one written request for resolution and the business has ignored you, stalled, or refused — it's time for a formal demand.

## What Should a Consumer Demand Letter Include?

A well-drafted consumer demand letter covers six elements. It identifies you and the business by full legal names. It describes the transaction — what you paid, when, and what was supposed to happen. It states the breach — what the business failed to do. It cites the legal basis for your claim, whether that's breach of contract, the Consumer Legal Remedies Act, or another statute. It specifies the remedy you're seeking — typically a dollar amount. And it sets a deadline, usually 14 to 30 days.

The tone should be factual, not angry. Courts may eventually see this letter. Every sentence should read like evidence, because it is.

## Does California Require a Demand Letter Before Filing a Lawsuit?

For claims under the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (CLRA), yes. Civil Code Section 1782 requires that you send the business a written demand at least 30 days before filing a CLRA lawsuit. The letter must describe the specific violations and the relief you're requesting. If you skip this step, a court can dismiss your CLRA claim.

For general breach-of-contract claims and small claims filings, a demand letter isn't technically required. But California small claims courts look favorably on plaintiffs who made a good-faith effort to resolve the dispute before filing. Judges notice when you show up with a paper trail that proves you gave the business every reasonable chance to make things right.

## How Often Do Demand Letters Actually Work?

No public database tracks the resolution rate of consumer demand letters in California, so anyone quoting a specific success percentage is guessing. What is observable: businesses respond to attorney-signed demand letters at dramatically higher rates than they respond to customer complaint emails. The letter changes the cost calculus. Ignoring a customer email costs nothing. Ignoring an attorney letter risks a lawsuit, court costs, and potentially statutory damages under the CLRA.

The cases where demand letters are least effective tend to involve businesses that are already insolvent, have closed, or are deliberately fraudulent. For operating businesses that simply haven't prioritized your refund, a demand letter frequently resolves the issue within two weeks.

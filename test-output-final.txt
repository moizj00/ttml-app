## II. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### A. Material Breach and Abandonment Under California Law

Your conduct constitutes a **material breach** of the Contract under well-established California law. The California Supreme Court has definitively held that a material breach is one that "goes to the essence of the contract" and "so totally defeats the purpose of the agreement that it is unjust to require the non-breaching party to continue performance." *Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman*, 51 Cal.4th 811, 821 (2011). A material breach excuses further performance by the non-breaching party and entitles her to terminate the contract and pursue full compensatory damages. *Id.*

The essence of the Contract was Pacific Coast Contractors LLC's obligation to complete a full kitchen renovation at the Property by July 15, 2025, in exchange for $85,000. Your abandonment of the project at merely 30% completion—followed by more than eight months of complete non-responsiveness to all forms of communication—constitutes a total failure of performance that defeats the fundamental purpose of the contractual relationship. Under the standard articulated in *Oasis West Realty*, this material breach entitles Ms. Mitchell to treat the Contract as terminated, recover all damages proximately caused by your breach, and pursue every available legal and administrative remedy.

Your unilateral cessation of work, coupled with your categorical refusal to communicate, constitutes an unequivocal **anticipatory repudiation** of your remaining contractual obligations, triggering Ms. Mitchell's immediate right to seek full compensatory relief without awaiting the original completion date.

### B. Damages Recoverable Under California Civil Code

California Civil Code Section 3300 provides that a party injured by breach of contract is entitled to recover "the amount which will compensate the party aggrieved for all the detriment proximately caused thereby, or which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result therefrom." Section 3301 confirms that a breach occurs when a party fails to perform an obligation arising from contract.

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to recover the following categories of damages:

#### 1. Refund of Unearned Deposit

Of the $42,500 deposit paid, Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has earned compensation only for the approximately 30% of work actually performed. Applying a proportional calculation to the total contract price:

- Value of work completed (30% × $85,000): **$25,500**
- Unearned portion of deposit: **$42,500 − $25,500 = $17,000**

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to the immediate refund of no less than **$17,000**, representing the unearned deposit for work never performed. Ms. Mitchell expressly reserves the right to contest the valuation of work purportedly completed—particularly given the absence of any documentation from Pacific Coast Contractors LLC regarding actual labor and materials expended—and to seek additional recovery if the work performed is determined to be defective, non-code-compliant, or of lesser value than the proportional calculation assumes.

#### 2. Cost to Complete the Contracted Work

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to recover the reasonable cost of engaging a replacement contractor to complete the remaining 70% of the contracted scope of work, less any credit for the unpaid balance of the original contract price. Cal. Civ. Code § 3300; *see also* Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 347 (cost of completion as standard measure of construction contract damages). Ms. Mitchell is presently obtaining competitive bids from licensed California contractors and will supplement this demand with specific figures upon receipt of those bids. To the extent the cost to complete exceeds the remaining contract balance of $42,500, Pacific Coast Contractors LLC will be liable for the difference.

#### 3. Consequential Damages

The $3,200 Ms. Mitchell has expended on temporary kitchen arrangements constitutes foreseeable consequential damages fully recoverable under Civil Code Section 3300. It is entirely foreseeable—indeed, virtually certain—that abandonment of a kitchen renovation mid-project would render the kitchen completely unusable and necessitate alternative arrangements for food preparation and meals. These damages flow directly and naturally from your breach and are recoverable as a matter of law. *See* Cal. Civ. Code § 3300 (damages "which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result" from breach are compensable).

#### 4. Prejudgment Interest

Ms. Mitchell is entitled to prejudgment interest at the statutory rate of **ten percent (10%) per annum** on all liquidated damages from the date of breach (May 20, 2025) through the date of judgment or payment. Cal. Civ. Code § 3289(b). As of the date of this letter, prejudgment interest on the $17,000 unearned deposit alone exceeds $1,275 and continues to accrue daily.

#### 5. Attorney's Fees and Costs

To the extent the Contract contains a prevailing party attorney's fees provision—as is standard in California construction contracts—Ms. Mitchell will seek recovery of all reasonable attorney's fees and litigation costs incurred in enforcing her rights pursuant to California Civil Code Section 1717. Additionally, Business and Professions Code Section 7168 may provide for fee recovery in actions arising from home improvement contract violations.

### C. Alternative Remedy: Rescission and Full Restitution

In the alternative to damages, Ms. Mitchell may elect to rescind the Contract pursuant to California Civil Code Section 1689(b)(4), which expressly permits rescission where the consideration for a party's obligation "fails, in whole or in part, through the fault of the party as to whom [the obligation] is owed." Your abandonment of the project at 30% completion constitutes a substantial failure of consideration entitling Ms. Mitchell to rescind the Contract and recover full restitution of the $42,500 deposit, subject only to a reasonable offset for any value actually conferred. Ms. Mitchell reserves the right to elect this remedy at any time prior to judgment.

### D. Statute of Limitations — Claim Fully Timely

This claim is timely under all applicable limitations periods. The statute of limitations for breach of a written contract in California is **four years** pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 337(a). The breach occurred on May 20, 2025, when Pacific Coast Contractors LLC abandoned the project and ceased performance. Fewer than twelve months have elapsed since that date. Ms. Mitchell's claims remain fully actionable, and she possesses more than three years within which to commence litigation if necessary.

## III. RESPONSE TO ANTICIPATED DEFENSES

Ms. Mitchell anticipates that Pacific Coast Contractors LLC may attempt to assert the following defenses, each of which is factually unsupported and legally unavailing:

### 1. "Partial Performance Justifies Retention of the Full Deposit."

**This defense fails.** While a breaching contractor may be entitled to quantum meruit recovery for the reasonable value of work actually performed, this principle does not excuse material breach, does not justify retention of funds grossly exceeding the value of work provided, and does not shield a contractor from liability for damages caused by abandonment. Ms. Mitchell has already credited Pacific Coast Contractors LLC with the estimated value of work completed ($25,500) in calculating the unearned deposit. Any claim to compensation beyond this amount must be supported by contemporaneous documentation of actual labor hours, material costs, and subcontractor invoices—none of which Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has provided despite eight months of opportunity and repeated requests for communication. Absent such documentation, no additional credit is warranted.

### 2. "The Homeowner Failed to Mitigate Her Damages."

**This defense fails.** Ms. Mitchell has acted reasonably, diligently, and in complete good faith throughout this dispute. She made extensive efforts to contact Pacific Coast Contractors LLC and resolve the matter amicably before seeking alternative contractors or legal remedies. California law requires only *reasonable* mitigation efforts—not immediate replacement of a breaching contractor while the homeowner is still attempting to salvage the contractual relationship and obtain performance. *See* Valle de Oro Bank v. Gamboa, 26 Cal.App.4th 1686, 1691 (1994) (mitigation requires only reasonable efforts under the circumstances). Ms. Mitchell's conduct has been exemplary, and any suggestion to the contrary is without factual or legal foundation.

### 3. "Force Majeure, Supply Chain Issues, or Owner-Caused Delays Excused Performance."

**This defense fails.** The undisputed facts demonstrate unilateral abandonment by Pacific Coast Contractors LLC—not delay caused by external circumstances, force majeure events, or homeowner interference. Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has identified no force majeure event, supply chain disruption, or other circumstance that would excuse its complete cessation of work and eight months of non-communication. Ms. Mitchell did not interfere with, delay, or obstruct the work in any manner. To the contrary, she has consistently sought to facilitate completion of the project. This defense is wholly inapplicable to the facts at hand.

### 4. "The Contract's Mediation Clause Bars Litigation."

**This defense fails.** While the Contract may contain a mediation provision, Pacific Coast Contractors LLC has rendered mediation impossible through its categorical refusal to respond to any form of communication for over eight months. A party cannot invoke a contractual dispute resolution mechanism as a procedural shield after its own conduct has made that process futile. *See* Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc., 15 Cal.4th 951, 983 (1997) (party may waive contractual dispute resolution rights through conduct). Ms. Mitchell has more than satisfied any obligation to attempt pre-litigation resolution; Pacific Coast Contractors LLC's prolonged silence constitutes waiver of any mediation requirement. Moreover, California courts have consistently held that a party's refusal to participate in contractual dispute resolution excuses the other party's compliance with such provisions.

### 5. "The Claimed Damages Are Speculative or Excessive."

**This defense fails.** Ms. Mitchell's claimed damages are conservative, well-documented, and fully supported by California law. The $17,000 unearned deposit refund is a straightforward mathematical calculation that already credits Pacific Coast Contractors LLC for work performed. The $3,200 in consequential damages is supported by itemized receipts and represents the foreseeable cost of temporary arrangements necessitated by an unusable kitchen. The cost-to-complete damages will be established by competitive bids from licensed contractors. Nothing in this demand is speculative; to the contrary, Ms. Mitchell has been conservative in her calculations and has reserved claims for additional damages that may be warranted upon further investigation.
